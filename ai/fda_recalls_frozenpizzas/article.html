Important: A Story About Pizza, Safety, and Looking Out for Each Other

By Charles Gilchrist

Published: December 13, 2024

Last night, as I was pulling a frozen pizza out of my freezer (my go-to comfort food after a long day), I got some news that made me think about how something as simple as a pizza connects us all. Whether you're a student cramming for finals, a parent trying to put together a quick dinner, or just someone who appreciates the simple pleasure of a hot slice, frozen pizza has become part of our shared American story.

Today, I need to share something important about that story, especially if you live in Illinois, Minnesota, or Wisconsin. It's about Connie's Thin Crust Cheese Pizza, a familiar name in many Midwestern freezers.

A Pizza Story That Matters

I've spent time in pizza joints across the country, from fancy wood-fired ovens in New York to beloved frozen pizza factories in the Midwest. One thing I've learned is that making food for others is a responsibility that good companies take seriously. That's why I was actually impressed when I heard that Palermo Villa, Inc. (the folks behind Connie's) decided to recall some of their pizzas.

Here's what happened: During their quality checks, they found something that didn't belong - small pieces of plastic. Instead of crossing their fingers and hoping for the best, they immediately raised their hands and said, "We need to fix this." It reminds me of what my grandmother always said: "When you're cooking for others, you cook like you're cooking for family."

What You Need to Know

Let me break this down simply: if you've got a Connie's Thin Crust Cheese Pizza in your freezer (the 20.36 oz size), take a moment to check it. Look for these numbers on the box:

The lot number to look for is MR199856, and the "best by" date is 11/27/2024. Think of it like checking the expiration date on milk - it's just something we do to keep ourselves and our families safe.

The Good News

There's actually some really good news in this story. Nobody has gotten sick or reported any problems. The company caught this early - kind of like how my friend Maria spots problems in her family restaurant before they ever reach a customer. It's what caring food makers do.

What to Do If You Have One

If you find one of these pizzas in your freezer, here's what you should do: Don't eat it (even though it might be tempting), but don't throw it away either. You can take it back to the store where you bought it for a full refund. Think of it as a rain check for a future pizza night.

A Helping Hand

The folks at Palermo's want to help if you have questions. They've set up a special phone line: 1-888-571-7181. You can call them Monday through Friday, 8:00 AM to 4:30 PM CST. I've actually called them myself while writing this article - they're real people who genuinely want to help.

The Bigger Slice

You know what I love about this whole situation? It shows how our food system, when it works right, is built on trust and responsibility. In my travels across the country, I've learned that whether it's a small family pizzeria or a large company like Palermo's, the good ones always put safety first.

This recall isn't just about plastic in pizza - it's about people looking out for each other. It's about a company saying, "We can do better," and taking action to protect their customers. In a world that often feels disconnected, it's a reminder that we're all still looking out for each other, one pizza at a time.
